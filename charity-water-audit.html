02 — Scope & Focus

What I audited and why

I focused the audit on three pages that represent the full arc of a donor's decision journey:

  • Homepage — the first impression. Sets the tone, communicates the mission, and either earns or loses the user's attention in the first few seconds.
  • Donate page — the most critical page on the site. Any friction here directly translates to lost donations and unfulfilled mission impact.
  • Get Involved / Fundraise page — the depth play. For users who want to go beyond a one-time donation, this page either deepens their relationship with the organization or loses them.

The audit covered five dimensions: visual design, heuristic evaluation, accessibility, content quality, and information architecture. Each was evaluated independently and then synthesized into cross-cutting findings.

Primary audit question

Does the charity: water website make it easy for a first-time visitor to understand the mission, feel confident about the organization, and complete a donation — or does the design get in the way?

— Audit objective

03 — Visual Design

Strong foundation, a few cracks

Overall the visual design is polished and professional. The layout, typography, and color usage are all at a high level for a nonprofit site. But a few specific patterns create friction that the clean aesthetic masks.

✓ What works

Clear section structure makes content scannable

Consistent grid with strong alignment throughout

Typography hierarchy is clear — headings, subheadings, and body text are well differentiated

Color contrast is WCAG compliant in most text areas

Buttons and CTAs are consistently styled and easy to identify

✗ What doesn't

Multiple storytelling sections use nearly identical layouts — users lose their sense of where they are while scrolling

Large photos placed near CTAs occasionally pull attention away from the action

Some emphasized text and buttons rely solely on color to signal importance — no secondary cue for color-blind users

Some colored text appears clickable but isn't, creating false affordances

Small decorative icons sit too close to surrounding text, especially on mobile

The most important visual issue

The false affordance problem — colored text that looks like a link but isn't — is the one I'd prioritize fixing. It's a direct trust issue. On a site where donor confidence is everything, an element that looks interactive but does nothing trains users to distrust the interface.

04 — Heuristic Evaluation

Where the experience breaks Nielsen's principles

I evaluated the site against Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics. Most hold up well — but four specific violations stand out as having real impact on donors.

High impact

Consistency & standards violated — Legacy section breaks the experience

Clicking "Legacy" redirects users to a completely different URL with a different navigation structure and visual design. Users lose their mental model of the site entirely, and there's no obvious way back to the main site. "Home" navigates to the Legacy homepage, not the main charity: water homepage.

High impact

User control & freedom — no back button in donation flow

The multi-step donation flow has no back button. Users who want to change their donation amount or ticket type after advancing a step have no escape route other than the browser back button, which may reset their progress entirely.

Medium impact

Visibility of system status — no progress indicator in checkout

The donation flow moves through multiple steps (amount → personal info → payment → review) but users are never told how many steps exist or which step they're on. First-time donors in particular are left guessing how much longer the process will take.

Medium impact

Efficiency of use — repeat donors get the same experience as first-timers

Even logged-in users go through the full story-heavy homepage and the same multi-step checkout rather than a faster authenticated flow. Returning donors who already understand the mission are forced to navigate through content designed to convince strangers.

05 — UX Laws Applied

Naming the principles behind the problems

Every finding in this audit maps to an established UX principle. Naming them isn't just academic — it's how you move from "this feels wrong" to "here's why it's wrong and here's the evidence-backed fix."

Law Miller's Law

No progress indicator in the donation flow

Miller's Law states that users can only hold 7 (±2) items in working memory at once. When users don't know how many steps remain in a checkout process, they have to expend cognitive resources tracking their position instead of focusing on completing the task. A simple "Step 2 of 3" indicator offloads that tracking entirely — freeing mental capacity for the decision itself.

Law Hick's Law

"Get Involved" and "Take Action" as separate navigation items

Hick's Law states that the time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and similarity of choices. Two navigation labels that mean nearly the same thing don't clarify the choice — they double the uncertainty. Card sort participants consistently struggled to differentiate them, confirming that the similarity is creating decision paralysis rather than helpfully organizing content.

Law Jakob's Law

Legacy section breaking navigation consistency + repeat donor experience

Jakob's Law states that users spend most of their time on other sites, so they expect your site to work the same way as the ones they already know. The Legacy redirect violates this twice: it breaks the navigation pattern users just learned on the main site, and it removes the "Home" escape route they rely on. Returning donors are also affected — they've built a mental model of the internet's standard donation flow, and being forced through a story-heavy homepage each visit violates that expectation.

Law Law of Affordance

Colored non-clickable text that looks like a link

Affordances communicate what an object can do. Colored text on the web has one strong affordance: it's a link, and it's clickable. When that expectation is violated — when colored text goes nowhere — users learn to distrust the interface. On a site where donor confidence is critical, anything that erodes trust has a cost that's disproportionate to how small the design error seems.

Law Fitts's Law

Subtle focus indicators and small interactive targets on the donation form

Fitts's Law states that the time to reach a target is a function of its size and distance. On the donation form, elements that are small or visually unclear — like the nearly invisible keyboard focus indicator — increase the cost of interaction for all users, and make some interactions effectively impossible for keyboard and screen reader users.

Law Peak-End Rule

Payment failure experience and the "are you sure?" interstitial

The Peak-End Rule states that people judge an experience by its most intense moment and its ending — not the average. The donation flow's ending is its most important moment. An abrupt payment failure with no recovery guidance, or an "are you sure you don't want to give monthly?" interstitial right before completion, makes the peak and end of the experience feel adversarial rather than grateful. That's the memory donors take away.

06 — Accessibility

Good intent, inconsistent execution

There's a particular irony in a humanitarian organization's website having accessibility gaps. charity: water's mission is about reaching people others have overlooked — that principle should extend to how the site treats users with disabilities.

High impact

Donation form fields announced incorrectly by screen readers

The donation amount selector announces "100, USD, mo" as three separate items rather than as a single composed field. A screen reader user navigating the form hears a confusing sequence of values without context for how they relate. The fields also don't clearly signal that they're interactive.

Medium impact

Focus indicators are too subtle for keyboard-only users

Keyboard navigation works technically, but the visual focus indicator on many elements is so subtle it's nearly invisible. A user navigating without a mouse has almost no visual feedback about which element is currently focused — making the "past years" date selector completely unusable via keyboard.

Lower impact

Storytelling images may lack descriptive alt text

Several large storytelling images — which are central to the site's emotional appeal — may not have descriptive alt text. Screen reader users miss the emotional context that sighted users get from those images, which is a significant gap on a site that relies heavily on visual storytelling.

07 — Information Architecture

Card sorting revealed a navigation labeling problem

To test whether the site's navigation structure matched users' mental models, I ran a closed card sorting exercise with 2 participants. They were given content cards representing pages and features and asked to sort them into the existing navigation categories.

What I found

Key finding

"Get Involved" and "Take Action" are indistinguishable

Both participants struggled to differentiate these two navigation categories and placed cards inconsistently between them. The labels are nearly synonymous to a first-time visitor — they suggest the same type of action without distinguishing what's actually different about each section.

Key finding

Jargon labels like "Experience Lab" and "Stream for Us" confused participants

Internal terminology that makes sense to the charity: water team doesn't translate to first-time visitors. Participants couldn't predict what content lived under these labels without prior knowledge of the site — which means they'd either avoid them or explore inefficiently.

"I wasn't sure if 'Get Involved' and 'Take Action' were the same thing or different things. I kind of just picked one."

— Card sort participant

The underlying structure of the site is solid — participants understood the broad categories once they were explained. The problem is labeling, not architecture. Renaming "Take Action" to something more specific, like "Fundraise" or "Campaigns," would resolve most of the confusion.

08 — Content Evaluation

Compelling storytelling that occasionally gets in its own way

The content on charity: water is genuinely good — the tone is human, the mission is clear, and the emotional storytelling is effective. The problem is structural: the site optimizes for first-time visitors discovering the cause, but not for goal-oriented users who already want to donate.

✓ What works

Mission statement is immediately visible and clearly articulated

Conversational tone lowers the barrier for first-time donors

CTA buttons are concise and action-oriented ("Donate", "Give")

Impact statistics and transparency reports build donor confidence

No technical jargon — accessible to a broad audience

✗ What doesn't

Long narrative sections slow users who just want to donate

Donation prompts appear before users have enough context — creating hesitation rather than encouraging action

Error messages say "This field is required" without explaining what's wrong or how to fix it

Repeated CTAs throughout the page can feel pushy before trust is established

The "are you sure you don't want to donate monthly?" interstitial adds friction at the worst possible moment

09 — Recommendations

If I could change six things

Not all findings are equal. These are the changes I'd prioritize, ordered by impact on the primary goal: completing a donation.

Priority 1

Add a progress indicator to the donation flow

A simple "Step 2 of 3" indicator would reduce uncertainty and abandonment for first-time donors. It's the single highest-impact change for the lowest implementation cost.

Priority 2

Add a back button to every checkout step

Users need to feel in control of the process. Trapping them at a step with no back button creates anxiety — exactly the wrong emotion at the moment of financial commitment.

Priority 3

Fix the Legacy navigation break

The Legacy redirect to a different site with different navigation is a significant consistency violation. At minimum, ensure the "Home" link in the Legacy section returns to the main charity: water homepage.

Priority 4

Strengthen donation form accessibility

Fix the screen reader announcement of the donation amount field, improve focus indicators on interactive elements, and ensure all storytelling images have meaningful alt text.

Priority 5

Rename "Take Action" to something specific

"Fundraise", "Campaigns", or "Events" would instantly reduce navigation confusion without any structural change to the site.

Priority 6

Create a faster flow for returning donors

Logged-in users shouldn't need to scroll through mission content they already know. A streamlined authenticated donation flow with saved payment details would improve retention.

What this audit taught me

The most interesting tension I found in this project was between charity: water's two competing goals: inspiring new donors through emotional storytelling, and enabling goal-oriented users to donate quickly. These aren't naturally compatible — and the site's current design optimizes heavily for the first at the expense of the second.

It made me think carefully about how design decisions that are correct for one user type can create friction for another. The solution isn't to strip out the storytelling — it's what makes the site compelling. The solution is to create clearer pathways that let goal-oriented users bypass it when they want to. That's a design problem, not a content problem.
